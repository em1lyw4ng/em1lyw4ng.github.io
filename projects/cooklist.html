Empowering users with better visibility of their groceries to do more in the kitchen

Context

May 2024 – August 2024 / Product Management Intern / Python, Figma, Excel

Cooklist is an all-in-one application streamlining the way people meal plan, grocery shop, and cook. Their mission is to help people save money, reduce waste, and eat better by showing users what they can cook with what ingredients they have. I personally use Cooklist and its mission aligns with my interests and values.

As soon as I graduated in the spring of 2024, I specifically reached out to and was subsequently hired as an intern by Cooklist to imagine what an improved pantry management experience looks like.

Breaking Down the Problem

I spent a lot of time collecting, extracting, and visualizing both quantitative and qualitative data from over 2000+ users to understand where the digital pantry wasn't quite working.


We found that users fluctuate greatly between being proactive vs. reactive with managing the pantry. We identified common management behaviors and synthesized them into 3 personas:

Balancer: Found a routine between adding and using items, is motivated to keep their pantry updated because it helps them reduce waste and save money when grocery shopping


Stockpiler: Slowly consumes items since they like to know that their entire pantry is accounted for, or they prefer to explore what they can cook with all of their new purchases


Dropper: Maintains stagnant stock levels and shows an abrupt, reactive decline when they want a fresh start, some of these users might also not replenish their pantry and give up tracking items entirely


Users ubiquitously express "this app takes some time and devotion in order to work the way it’s intended" because burden is placed on them to cross-check their digital pantry with items in their actual home pantry. This includes sifting through items to verify that it's expired or relying on memory of what was used before deleting from the app.


Approximately 56% of our users are women ages 18-34, busy people who commonly demonstrated that adopting a new habit, especially one that requires effort, can be a big commitment for someone with little time. Thus, users who don't have the capacity for pantry upkeep, such as Droppers, feel overwhelmed and begin to view the app more as a task than a helpful kitchen assistant as the number of outdated or unverified items accumulate.


From watching others engage in pantry management, we also observed that importing items into the pantry—via barcode scanning or automatic imports—is comparatively more intuitive and efficient than removing. More data analysis revealed that 30% of users have a pantry size exceeding the average of 130 items, indicating that there’s a pain point with users keeping an accurate pantry.

Pantry System Update

We redesigned the pantry update system and improved the algorithm to identify likely expired or used items, additionally giving users the option to automatically remove them. The redesign emphasizes transparency and user control while reducing manual effort, and better keeps the pantry up-to-date with minimal effort from users.
